HOW TO BRIEF YOUR INTERIOR DESIGNER: A PRACTICAL GUIDE

May 10, 2026 5 min read By NextGen Team

After fifteen years and a few hundred projects, we’ve learned something blunt: most projects that go sideways don’t go sideways because of the designer — they go sideways at the brief. The bad news is, briefing is harder than it looks. The good news is, it’s a skill, and you can get good at it in an afternoon.

Here’s the version of the briefing checklist we wish every new client arrived with. Use it before your first meeting — you’ll save weeks of revision later, and tens of lakhs in changes that should have been caught before drawings began.

01START WITH HOW YOU LIVE, NOT HOW IT SHOULD LOOK

The single most common briefing mistake: leading with a Pinterest board. We get a folder of fifty beautiful rooms and zero information about the people who’ll actually use the space.

Before you talk about marble or moody lighting, write down the boring stuff. How many people live here? Who cooks, and how often? Do you eat at the dining table or in front of the TV? Do you work from home? Do your in-laws stay over — how often, for how long? Do you actually entertain, or do you just say you entertain?

A good designer can make almost any aesthetic work. What they can’t do is rescue a space designed for a life you don’t actually live.

02BRING 10 PHOTOS — AND EXPLAIN WHY EACH

Visual references are still essential. But here’s the rule that changes everything: for every image, write one sentence about why it’s in the folder.

Not “I love this kitchen.” That’s useless. Try: “I love how the island in this kitchen is a different stone from the counters — it makes the room feel less monolithic.”

Now the designer knows it’s about material contrast, not that specific stone. The same Pinterest board, with annotations, becomes ten times more useful.

A vague brief produces a beautiful design that’s wrong for you. A precise brief produces something you actually want to live in.

03BE HONEST ABOUT BUDGET

This is the awkward one. Clients often under-share their budget hoping for a lower quote, then are surprised when the proposal misses what they actually wanted. Or they share an aspirational number that doesn’t reflect what they’re willing to spend.

A useful budget conversation has three numbers:

  • Ideal: what you’d happily spend if everything aligns
  • Realistic: what you’ve actually allocated
  • Walk-away: the number above which you’d cancel the project

Share all three with your designer. A good one will work to your “realistic,” flag the trade-offs, and only push toward “ideal” on the items that genuinely justify it.

04LIST YOUR NON-NEGOTIABLES

Every project has two or three things the client absolutely must have, and a few things they absolutely don’t want. Get those on paper, day one.

“The kitchen must be open to the living room.” “No carpets anywhere.” “The bathtub stays even if we lose the second sink.” “No overhead fluorescents.” These are gifts to your designer — they prevent weeks of work going down a path you’d veto in five minutes.

Pre-Brief Checklist
  • Floor plan (rough is fine) with rooms labelled
  • One-paragraph description of who lives there and how
  • 10–15 reference images, each annotated with one sentence
  • Budget: ideal / realistic / walk-away
  • Three non-negotiables — things that must happen
  • Three deal-breakers — things that mustn’t happen
  • Hard timeline (move-in date, event, etc.) if any
  • Who’s the final decision-maker

05CHOOSE ONE DECISION-MAKER

The single fastest way to derail an interior project: two clients with veto power. Husband says yes, wife says no. Father funds, son chooses. Both bring different references on different days.

The fix is not to silence anyone — it’s to nominate one person as the “final yes.” Everyone gives input. One person decides. Communicate this to your designer up-front, and every meeting moves twice as fast.

If a single decision-maker isn’t possible, agree in writing on the few decisions that require both, and let one person own everything else. The worst version is when nobody knows whose call it is.

A FINAL NOTE

A good brief feels almost too clinical when you write it. You’re emotionally invested in the project, and reducing it to bullet points feels reductive. Do it anyway. The clarity you give your designer comes back to you as fewer revisions, fewer surprise costs, and a finished space that feels like your space — not a generic version of it.

The brief is the most leveraged hour you’ll spend on the entire project. Spend it well.
